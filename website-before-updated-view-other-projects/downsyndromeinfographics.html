The Situation

As a member of TNH, Syracuse University's student-run advertising agency, I was on the creative team for an advertising campaign for Gigi's Playhouse, a nonprofit that helps people with Down syndrome. I was tasked with designing two infographics: one that helped people learn about Down syndrome, and another that gave information about Gigi's Playhouse.

The Design Process

Considerations

Since I was designing for an actual organization, I had to make sure that I was following the style guide's approved colors, typefaces, logos, and photos. And while most of the copy I used in my infographics came directly from Gigi's Playhouse's website, I had to make sure that, for the copy I did write, I used language that was accurate, respectful, and inclusive of people with Down syndrome.

Infographic 1

For the infographic about Gigi's Playhouse, I chose to focus on their math tutoring service. I used different font weights and sizes to create visual hierarchy. I used a photo to draw interest, and made the list more interesting by making the bullet points plus signs to connect to the subject matter.

Infographic 2

For the infographic about Down syndrome, I chose to focus on how students with Down syndrome learn best. There was an article on Gigi's Playhouse's website focused on this, and I compressed that information down into an infographic that could be posted on Instagram as a carousel post. I chose two important parts of each section of the article and included them in pages corresponding to each section. Within the copy, I emphasized certain words (by increasing the font weight and changing the color) that I wanted to draw attention to.

Outcome

I designed two infographics for a non-profit organization and was one of three creatives awarded the title "Standout Creative Member"
